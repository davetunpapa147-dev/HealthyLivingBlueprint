What Is High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)? A Practical Guide

Medically reviewed by: [Dr. First Last, MD, Cardiology — Institution] — Last reviewed: YYYY-MM-DD

High blood pressure (hypertension) means the force of blood pushing against artery walls is higher than recommended. Hypertension increases risk of heart attack, stroke, kidney disease and other complications. This guide explains what numbers mean, common causes, tests, and evidence-based treatments and lifestyle strategies.

How blood pressure is measured

Systolic vs diastolic

Systolic is the pressure when the heart contracts; diastolic is the pressure when the heart relaxes. Current guideline thresholds vary by organization — see references.

Blood pressure categories

  • Normal: <120/80 mm Hg
  • Elevated: 120–129/<80 mm Hg
  • Stage 1 Hypertension: 130–139/80–89 mm Hg
  • Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥140/90 mm Hg

Proper measurement technique

Use a validated arm cuff; sit quietly 5 minutes; arm at heart level; take 2–3 readings and average. Use home or ambulatory measurement to confirm diagnosis if office values are borderline.

How common is hypertension?

Hypertension is common and prevalence increases with age. Many people remain undiagnosed or undertreated — routine screening is important.

Causes and types

Primary (essential) hypertension is the most common type—multifactorial and develops gradually. Secondary hypertension has an identifiable cause (e.g., kidney disease, endocrine disorders, medications, sleep apnea) and should be suspected with sudden onset or resistant hypertension.

Common contributing factors include high sodium intake, obesity, inactivity, excessive alcohol, poor sleep, and smoking.

Symptoms and long‑term complications

Most people have no symptoms; severe elevations may cause headache, vision changes or chest pain—seek urgent care if these occur. Untreated hypertension increases risk of heart attack, stroke, heart failure, kidney disease and vision loss.

Risk factors

  • Non-modifiable: age, family history, ethnicity
  • Modifiable: high-sodium diet, excess weight, inactivity, smoking, alcohol

Diagnosis: tests & follow-up

Confirm diagnosis with multiple readings; consider 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring for suspected white-coat or masked hypertension. Baseline labs often include: fasting glucose/A1C, lipid panel, kidney function, urinalysis and ECG.

Medical treatments (overview)

First-line antihypertensive classes include ACE inhibitors, ARBs, thiazide diuretics and calcium channel blockers; therapy is individualized based on comorbidities and risk. Medication decisions and targets should follow your clinician and guideline preference.

Lifestyle recommendations

  • Adopt DASH-style diet (see meal plan)
  • Reduce sodium; increase potassium-rich foods where safe
  • aim for 150 min/week moderate exercise; weight loss if needed
  • Improve sleep; screen for obstructive sleep apnea
  • Limit alcohol & stop smoking
  • Manage stress (mindfulness, breathing)

Evidence-based herbal supplements (brief)

Garlic, CoQ10 and omega‑3 have modest evidence for BP or other CV benefits; discuss with your clinician before starting supplements (interactions possible).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I feel when my BP is high?
No — hypertension is usually asymptomatic; regular checks are key.
How often should I check my BP?
At least annually if normal; more often if elevated or on treatment—follow your clinician’s plan.
Can lifestyle changes replace medication?
Sometimes for mild hypertension; many people will need medication. Individualized decisions required.
